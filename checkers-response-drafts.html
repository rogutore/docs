The Situation

A deal that shifted mid-conversation.

Chris Spencer reached out as a wholesale retail buyer — three CT stores, a café opening soon, three FL locations on the horizon. A straightforward pallet deal. Tokyo Coffee spent weeks building freight quotes for it.

Then the pitch changed. His latest message is about becoming our exclusive Amazon seller. Taking over the channel we built. Pressure-closing with a February deadline: allocate space to another product or move forward now.

The core tension

Tokyo Coffee manages its own Amazon US storefront. Handing it to a third party means losing control over pricing, listings, reviews, PPC, and brand positioning — potentially competing against our own inventory.

Two response drafts follow. One opens the door to a Zoom. One closes it for now and demands answers.

Due Diligence

What we don't know yet.

The original pitch was retail. Three CT stores, café coming soon, FL expansion. That's a clean, legible deal. But checkersretail.com is a thin WooCommerce template with no named locations. The real Checkers Food Store & Deli chain in CT uses a different domain entirely.

Chris's latest emails are now almost entirely about Amazon reseller volume — not retail shelves. And his Feb 23 deadline pressure ("allocate space to another product") arrived without a PO.

01
Is Italy Best Coffee related to Checkers? It came up in research as a major Amazon coffee seller. No answer yet.
02
Do the retail stores actually exist? checkersretail.com has no addresses, no photos, no proof of brick-and-mortar presence.
03
What is the real split between Amazon and retail? If this is 90% Amazon, the deal we've been building freight quotes for doesn't exist.
04
What does "exclusive" mean in practice? Are they asking us to shut down our own Amazon listing?
Two Drafts.

One opens a conversation. One demands clarity.

Draft A — Open the Door

Let's get on Zoom.

Tone: genuinely open, curious, not alarmed. Signals we take this seriously but need to understand it before we can commit. Keeps the relationship warm. Buys time without closing the door.

Strategic intent

We're not saying no. We're saying the arrangement is complex enough that email isn't the right format. A Zoom surfaces whether he's a serious operator or a sophisticated pitch artist. Low cost to us, high information value.

Draft A — Send if open to exploring
Re: Are we moving forward?

Hi Chris,

Thanks for sharing more about how you operate — it's a model we weren't fully familiar with coming in, and we appreciate you laying it out.

We're genuinely open to exploring this, but it's a meaningfully different arrangement from what we understood we were discussing, and there are enough moving parts that we'd rather talk through it properly than go back and forth over email.

Would you be up for a quick Zoom or call this week? We have some questions we want to work through before we can say whether this makes sense for us:

The main one is the competitive dynamic. We currently manage our own Tokyo Coffee storefront on Amazon US. If you're selling the same products under your Seller Central account, we'd effectively be competing against ourselves — at least initially, and possibly indefinitely. How do brands you work with typically handle that transition? Do they shut down their own listing, or do both run simultaneously?

We'd also want to understand the structure better. When you talk about being an "exclusive" Amazon seller — what does that mean in practice? Is the expectation that we step back from Amazon entirely and you take it over? Or is there a co-existence model?

On the numbers side, we'd want to get a clearer picture of what you project across channels. If you're also selling through your Connecticut stores, your café, your website, and Amazon, what does the split look like? What volume are we actually talking about per channel, and on what timeline?

One more question — there's a coffee company called Italy Best Coffee that came up in our research as a well-established Amazon seller in the coffee space. Is that related to your operation in any way?

Happy to send over some time slots if you're open to a call.

Best regards,
Noah
Tokyo Coffee

Draft B — Not Ready

We need answers first.

Tone: direct and clear, not hostile. Acknowledges the pivot explicitly. Refuses to be deadline-pressured. Leaves the door open conditionally, but makes clear what we'd need to see to walk through it.

Strategic intent

If the retail deal no longer exists and this is purely an Amazon grab, we need to know now before investing more time. This draft names the shift directly, declines to be pressured, and turns the pressure back: prove the business, answer the questions, then we'll talk.

Draft B — Send if not ready to engage
Re: Are we moving forward?

Hi Chris,

Thanks for following up. Before we can answer your question about moving forward, we need to get some clarity on what exactly is being proposed, because the conversation has shifted significantly from where it started.

You initially reached out about purchasing inventory for your retail stores. That's a straightforward wholesale arrangement and one we've been working hard to support — hence the freight quotes, the pricing structure, all of it. But your last message was about becoming our exclusive Amazon seller and having us step back from managing our own channel. That's a completely different proposition, and frankly not one we're ready to agree to.

So to answer your question directly: if the deal is contingent on handing over our Amazon presence to you, then no, we're not ready to move forward on that basis. We've built our Amazon US operation ourselves and it's our primary sales channel. Giving that up — or competing against our own inventory — is not something we'd do without a lot more information, trust built over time, and terms that genuinely work for us.

That said, we're not closing the door. But we have questions that need real answers first:

Is the retail deal still on the table independently? Or are you saying Amazon is now the only arrangement you're interested in?

If you're proposing an Amazon partnership, what does "exclusive" actually mean? Are you asking us to deactivate our own listings? Take down our storefront? Stop selling on Amazon ourselves?

What brands have you done this with, and can you share references or verifiable examples? You mentioned growing 10,000+ brands — we'd want to speak with one or two who can speak to the experience.

What are the projected volumes, and across which channels? You've mentioned retail stores, a café, a website, and Amazon. What percentage of sales goes through each, and what realistic monthly volumes are you projecting for Tokyo Coffee specifically?

Is Italy Best Coffee — which came up in our research as a major Amazon seller in coffee — part of your operation or affiliated with you in any way?

What would the contractual arrangement look like? Exclusivity terms, duration, minimum purchase commitments, what happens if targets aren't met?

We're a small, independent roaster and our brand equity on Amazon is something we've built carefully. We'd need to understand exactly what we'd be getting in return before we'd consider any change to how we operate that channel.

Best regards,
Noah
Tokyo Coffee

Key Insight

The deadline pressure is a tell. A real wholesale buyer sends a PO. A pitch artist sends an ultimatum.

Decision Framework

How to read his answer.

Whatever he says next will tell you everything about which draft was the right call.

01

He jumps on Zoom

Good sign. Real operators make time. Prepare the questions list and hold firm on Amazon channel terms.

02

He answers the questions in writing

Also fine. Read carefully for specifics. Vague references to volume without numbers = red flag.

03

He applies more pressure

"Final answer needed today" without addressing any questions = walk away. Send nothing further.

04

He goes silent

The deadline was theatre. Wait. If he comes back in a week without urgency, re-evaluate on cleaner terms.
