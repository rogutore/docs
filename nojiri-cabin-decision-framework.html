01 — The Situation

How We Got Here

The cabin project has stalled. Here's the sequence of events that brought us to this point.

Original Plan Submitted

A new-build design was developed and presented to the neighbors for approval.

Neighbor Rejection

Neighbors objected. Height was effectively capped at 6.5 meters—gutting much of the second floor/loft as viable living space.

Plans Changed, Costs Remained

The design was compromised, but the price tag stayed high: 45–55 million yen, with parents expected to contribute.

Family Consensus

We will not let our parents pay that much for a plan they are not fully satisfied with. If that's the situation, we would rather drop the project entirely.

New Rule

Kids should pay for everything. However, kids cannot afford a new build at that price point.

Impasse

Without a new path forward, we face years of stagnation—no progress, no cabin, fading energy.

The Family's Position

We refuse to let our parents pay a large sum for something that doesn't serve them well. That's the line. If we can't find a path that makes sense for everyone, we'd rather let it go.

02 — The Real Question

What Are We Actually Deciding?

This decision is no longer about square meters, insulation specs, or architectural perfection.

It's About

Who gets to enjoy the cabin. When they get to enjoy it. And at what emotional and financial cost to the family.

The Scenario We Cannot Accept

A future where we wait until our parents are too old to want to go, half the kids have left home, and then we use their money to build the "perfect" cabin. That cabin would no longer be perfect at all. It would be empty of the people and moments that give it meaning.

The Core Question

If we put "bigger, better cabin later" on one side of a scale, and "the value of shared years now" on the other—how do we weigh them? That's what we need to talk through together.

03 — The Options

Four Paths Forward

Each option has trade-offs. None is perfect. All are worth understanding.

Option A — Renovation
Constrained but Clean
Budget
¥10–12M (hard cap)
Who Pays
Kids only
Timeline
Soon

Concept

Renovate the existing structure. Same footprint, squared-off back side. Prioritize safety, warmth, and cost containment.

What's Included

  • Jack up and level the building
  • Repair or add foundation stilts
  • Replace all rotten beams and joists
  • Full insulation: floors, walls, ceilings (~9cm)
  • Double-pane windows
  • Water and sewage

Limitations

  • No budget for roof work
  • Much smaller than original plan
  • Not suitable for family Christmas or snow camp
  • Limited long-term hosting capacity

Financially clean and low risk—but emotionally constrained. A cabin, but not the gathering place we imagined.

Option B — Simplified New Build
Imperfect but Alive
Budget
¥30M (hard cap)
Who Pays
Parents (if aligned)
Timeline
Soon

Why This Option Exists

This isn't "parents fund our dream cabin." This is a plan that finally makes sense for them. Almost half the original cost. A space they can actually use. Time with kids and grandkids while everyone is still present.

Key Requirement

  • Parents have their own private room. This is firm. It's why we would consider their contribution again—because this plan serves them, not just us.

Layout Concept

  • First floor: 2–3 simple rooms, including parents' room
  • Upper level: large open loft—everyone sleeps together, no individual rooms
  • Lakeside: open vertical space for scale without violating height rules
  • Deck absorbed into interior space (possibly no deck initially)

Build Philosophy

  • Simple, low-cost foundation
  • Lower-spec insulation and windows vs. original dream
  • Still warm, code-compliant, brand new, highly usable

The Covenant

  • Kids do everything possible to contribute and reduce burden
  • Noah guarantees parents' financial security
  • Miura house money hack or other mechanisms if needed
  • No scenario where parents are left vulnerable

Imperfect—but alive, shared, and timely. A place where memories actually happen.

Option C — Wait / Do Nothing
Highest Opportunity Cost
Budget
¥0
Who Pays
—
Timeline
Indefinite

Concept

Pause everything. Hope for a Sedera miracle or NLA rule change down the road.

Reality

  • Likely outcome: years of stagnation
  • Kids age out
  • Parents age out
  • Energy and momentum fade

Lowest action, lowest immediate cost—but potentially the highest cost in lost years and fading opportunity.

Option D — Force Original Plan
High Effort, Uncertain Payoff
Budget
¥45–55M
Who Pays
Parents
Timeline
This summer (attempt)

Concept

Make one final push for the original plan this summer. If rejected again, fall back to Option A or B.

Pros

  • No lingering "what if we had tried?"
  • Emotional closure either way

Cons

  • Significant time and energy drain
  • Potential emotional exhaustion
  • May simply delay the inevitable choice

Worth considering if closure matters—but recognize the cost of the attempt itself.

04 — The Core Tradeoff

What Are We Really Weighing?

Underneath the specs and budgets, there's a simpler question.

"Perfect Later"

  • Bigger, better cabin
  • Built when circumstances align
  • Possibly after parents are older
  • Possibly after kids have moved on
  • When desire and energy may be lower

"Meaningful Now"

  • Smaller, simpler cabin
  • Built while it still matters
  • Filled with parents
  • Filled with kids and grandkids
  • Memories that actually happen

Which one is truly more "perfect"?

05 — Additional Context

Other Factors Worth Knowing

Noah's Potential NLA Membership

Noah may become a Nojiri Lake Association member this summer. If that happens, he would purchase his own separate cabin within the NLA community.

What this means for the family cabin:

  • One less family needing space in the shared cabin
  • Reduces hosting pressure and capacity requirements
  • Makes a smaller cabin more reasonable and viable

Financial Stewardship

Whatever we decide, we want to avoid consuming all of our parents' resources. Their safety, security, and dignity matter more than any cabin. The "Miura house money hack" and other mechanisms exist to ensure no scenario where they are left vulnerable.

Guiding Principle

Harmony over optimization. The goal is not to find the theoretically best cabin. It's to find a path we can all walk together—without resentment, without regret, without silent bitterness.

"Do nothing out of selfish ambition or vain conceit. Rather, in humility value others above yourselves, not looking to your own interests but each of you to the interests of the others."
— Philippians 2:3-4

Whatever we decide, let it be marked by this. Not "who wins" but "what serves us all." Not architectural ego, but family harmony. Not optimization, but unity.

If we hold this posture, we can't really get it wrong.
